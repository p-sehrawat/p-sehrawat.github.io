1. Crust:


Composition:

The Earth's crust is a thin, solid shell consisting of various rocks, primarily granitic and basaltic in nature. The continental crust is composed of lighter, granitic rocks, while the oceanic crust is denser and mainly consists of basalt.

Characteristics:

  • Thickness and Density: The crust varies in thickness, with continental crust being thicker (around 30-50 km) compared to oceanic crust (approximately 5-10 km). Continental crust is less dense than oceanic crust.
  • Landforms: Major geological features like mountains, valleys, plains, and coastlines are found on the crust.
  • Ecosystems: The crust supports diverse ecosystems, from forests to deserts, where life flourishes.

2. Mantle:


Composition:

The mantle is primarily composed of solid rock, dominated by minerals like peridotite. While mostly solid, the mantle has regions where rocks can flow over long periods.

Characteristics:

  • Convection Currents: Heat from the Earth's interior drives convection currents in the mantle, leading to the movement of tectonic plates.
  • Plates and Boundaries: The mantle's convection currents contribute to the creation and movement of lithospheric plates, impacting Earth's surface features.
  • Asthenosphere: The upper part of the mantle, called the asthenosphere, is partially molten and plays a critical role in the mobility of tectonic plates.

3. Outer Core:


Composition:

The outer core is composed mainly of liquid iron and nickel, with temperatures high enough to keep these materials in a molten state.

Characteristics:

  • Magnetic Field Generation: The movement of molten iron in the outer core generates the Earth's magnetic field through the geodynamo effect.
  • Convection in Liquid Metal: Heat-driven convection currents in the liquid outer core play a crucial role in sustaining the Earth's magnetic field.

4. Inner Core:


Composition:

The inner core is composed of solid iron and nickel due to extreme pressure, even though temperatures here are extremely high.

Characteristics:

  • Solid State: Despite the high temperatures, the inner core remains solid due to immense pressure.
  • Structural Integrity: The solid inner core contributes to the Earth's overall structural stability.
